Mental Health in the South Asian Community

by Farihah Begum Shukie

Trigger/content warning: seasonal affective disorder, generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder, mental health mentions.


With fall right around the corner here on the East Coast in the States, I am taking caution to take care of my mental health. Although I haven't been diagnosed, I have a strong feeling that I developed Seasonal Affective Disorder once I moved from a big city to a quiet suburb near the beach. I always loved winter when I was younger especially growing up in NYC. The holiday season was especially spectacular with all the gorgeous lights and decorations. Nowadays, living near the Jersey Shore has made summer my new favorite time of the year. During the winters here, I get depressed, lonely, and homesick.


Of course, this is definitely not the first time in my life I had to deal with a mental health issue. In college, I was diagnosed with General Anxiety Disorder and Panic Disorder. It made sense. Even though I mostly enjoyed what I was learning in college, the pressures of being an engineering student made me feel anxious most of the time and I had a panic attack before every exam in the first three years.


That was the first time in my life I decided to take care of my mental health. I made a conscious effort to sleep well, to eat healthier, to practice more yoga and meditation, and perhaps the most important thing I did was seeing a therapist. While I still hit many road bumps in preventing my panic attacks, all of these things helped my anxiety immensely.


But perhaps the hardest thing about going through this period of my life was having to tell my parents about it. In South Asian culture, taking care of your mental health is taboo. People donâ€™t want to address mental health issues because they don't think it exists. When I told my parents I was going to therapy they almost seemed like they didn't believe me. Why do I need to see a professional just because I get butterflies before an exam? Even though I cared about what they thought, I knew that my anxiety was real. Furthermore, I knew I wanted to be an engineer, to get my degree with my mental health intact. So I kept going even though I felt their judgment.


One morning I remember waking up knowing full well I had an exam to take that I had studied for days in advance. But that morning I couldn't remember one thing I studied. My mind went blank and my hands were shaking. I was having a panic attack but I didn't know how to get rid of it so I went on with my day like normal. I was quietly trying to eat breakfast at the table when suddenly my father put his hand on my head to wish me all the best for my exam. Suddenly, I couldn't take it anymore and I started to cry. I started to hug him (something we didn't often do in my household) and I felt a rush of sadness inundate me. My parents were the two people I went to most often whenever I was facing a problem. If they don't believe what I am going through is real then who will?




From that day on my parents always asked to make sure I continued therapy. I think seeing me panic scared them into realizing that panic attacks are real, mental health issues are real. From then on until the beginning of the senior year I called my dad before every exam to listen to his voice. His voice and words of encouragement soothed my mind and put me at ease. Somehow I was able to pass almost every exam, which in turn I was able to get my degree and still maintain excellent grades. Sometimes I look back and I wonder how I was able to do that even though my panic attacks were so intense.


I wonder if without my parents' support if I could have achieved what I did. To be honest I do think it would have been possible, but definitely much harder. I consider myself to be lucky to have parents who could put aside their cultural beliefs because they wanted to make sure their daughter was okay. I sincerely hope that more South Asian parents or parents from any culture, for that matter, can do that for their children. Anxiety is indeed very real and speaking up about it should not be taboo.
